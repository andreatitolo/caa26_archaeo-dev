archaeo.social & archaeo.dev

Decentralised, collective digital infrastructure for archaeology

Reclaiming the scholarly social web

In November 2022, the microblogging service Twitter (now X) was acquired by billionaire Elon Musk, triggering the first of several exoduses of its users. Amongst those who left the site for other platforms, or who simply stopped using it, were archaeologists and other scientists. ‘Science Twitter’ had been a prominent venue for scholarly communication on social media, providing a platform for researchers to talk directly to each other and to the public (Insall 2023). Its sudden disintegration was a wake-up call for many, highlighting the risks of entrusting public scientific discourse to a single private corporation. Subsequent events have only reinforced academia's need for digital services that are resilient to corporate capture, ‘enshittification’, and data colonialism (Brembs et al. 2023). And yet much of the digital infrastructure we rely upon as archaeologists—not just in terms of social media and communication, but software development, code sharing, collaboration, and so on—remains concentrated with a few large corporations.

archaeo.social and archaeo.dev are scholarly collectives that maintain decentralised digital services for archaeologists. We use free and open source software to promote a ‘do it yourself’ approach to digital research and scholarly communication tools, with the aim of breaking our field's dependence on centralised, proprietary platforms. Our services are used by and supported by hundreds of individual users and a number of scholarly societies. Though ‘free as in freedom’, maintaining these services does require an investment in money, time, labour, and skills in system administration – creating a significant barrier to entry for individual scholars. The aim of archaeo.dev is to overcome this barrier by pooling our resources and operating these tools collectively. We invite all members of the CAA community to use them, and help us build them further!

References

  • Brembs, Björn, Adrian Lenardic, Peter Murray-Rust, Leslie Chan, and Dasapta Erwin Irawan. 2023. Mastodon over Mammon: Towards Publicly Owned Scholarly Knowledge. Royal Society Open Science 10 (7): 230207. https://doi.org/10.1098/rsos.230207.
  • Insall, Robert. 2023. Science Twitter – Navigating Change in Science Communication. Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology 24 (5, 5): 305–6. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41580-023-00581-3.

The Fediverse

Mastodon is a free and open-source software platform for decentralized social networking and microblogging. It is one of the main alternative to Twitter/X. archaeo.social is a Mastodon instance for archaeologists, ancient historians and anyone interested in scholarly communication about the human past.

Join at: archaeo.social

Matrix Chat

Matrix is a free and open-source, decentralized, instant-messaging platform. It is one of the main alternatives to Whatsapp or Slack, with a focus on user privacy and encryption. Communities such as Young Researchers in Archaeometry are already present on our matrix server.

Join at: chat.archaeo.social

Git Forge

Forgejo is a git forge and a software development platform. It is a free and open-source, collectively-governed, alternative to GitHub. Forgejo provides a features-packed collaboration platform for developers with an intuitive and familiar UI. A FOSS platform is the idea companion to Open Science.

Join at: forge.archaeo.dev

Webring

If your eyes landed on this section first, congrats! you are old enough to remember the web 1.0. Webrings were a way to connect websites that shared a similar theme. This webring can also help in discovering new archaeology blogs around the web. Come join us, we have some hand-crafted old-style buttons too!

Join at: webring.archaeo.social

Collaborative Editing

Hedgedoc is an open-source, web-based, collaborative note-taking editor in Markdown format and a lightweight alternative to Google Docs. Hedgedoc can be used to easily collaborate with others on notes, graphs, and even presentations, all in real time.

Join at: md.archaeo.dev
